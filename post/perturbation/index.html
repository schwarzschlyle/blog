Perturbation Theory

Nature is simple in the most complicated way possible. Even if the most fundamental equations are elegantly simple, the resulting complexity arising from a system of practical interest could not be ignored. For instance, finding an analytic solution to a ($N=2$)-body problem in classical mechanics is a trivial problem set for undergrads while an incremental increase in the degrees of freedom to an ($N=3$)-body problem resisted centuries of analytic methods and was famous for “giving Newton a painful headache.” The problem was later tackled using chaos theory and nonlinear dynamics.

Deriving a theoretical framework by postulating a governing equation is one thing; unpacking it to reveal its physical essence and the extent of its implications is another. Analytic solutions of governing equations provide way more insights to a given phenomena especially to theory-building yet it is rarely an easy task or even downright impossible. There is even a million-dollar prize to “simply” proving that a fluid mechanics equation (the infamous Navier-Stokes Equations) could be analytically unpacked at all.

Analytic solutions are considered precious in a sense that they provide us with a playground to explore different relationships within the quantity they govern allowing one to possible bridge it to other mathematically-consistent theories. However, the equations of interest are rarely analytically solvable and the usual angle-of-attack is to plug it into an appropriate numerical solver such as RK4 or a finite-differencing scheme. One powerful method in a physicist’s toolkit is perturbation theory which, even if considered to be a subject of a plethora of memes (sin(x) = x, right?), provides a cheap way to produce an insightful solution in a the domain of the approximation scheme.

Perturbation methods provide a general line of attack to different forms of equations. Stripped to its essence, the main idea is to start from a well-known analytic solution $S_0$ of a model and consider the desired event of interest $S$ to be a perturbation $S_i$ of the well-known solution such that $S \approx S_0 + \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} \varepsilon^i S_i$. Then, the resulting perturbative expansion of the solution is truncated as

$$ S = S_0 + \sum_{i=1}^{n} \varepsilon^i S_i + \mathcal{O}(\varepsilon^{n+1}) $$

Such a truncation is considered to be an $n$th order perturbative solution.

Perturbative Expansion Process

... close enough to the unperturbed algebraic solution

To demonstrate mechanical process of perturbation expansion, consider the following transcendental algebraic equations

$$ x^3 - \varepsilon e^{-x} + 8 = 0 $$

One can view this as $(x^3 + 8) + (\varepsilon e^{-x}) = 0$ to be suggestive that $\varepsilon e^{-x}$ may be viewed as a perturbation of the original equation $x^3 + 8 = 0$. The solution to the transcendental equation can be expressed as a perturbative expansion $x = x_0 + \sum_{i=1}^{n-1} \varepsilon^i x_i + \mathcal{O}(\varepsilon^n)$[^first] where, here, $x_0 = -2$ is a known analytic solution to the unperturbed system $x^3 + 8 = 0$. The first step is plugging in the expansion ansatz to the equation giving us

$$ \left(x_0 + \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} \varepsilon^i x_i\right)^3 - \varepsilon \left(\text{exp}\left(-\left(x_0 + \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} \varepsilon^i x_i\right)\right)\right) + 8 = 0 $$

Then, we Taylor expand the function $x^3$ as

$$ \left(x_0 + \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} \varepsilon^i x_i\right)^3 = \sum_{m=0}^\infty \frac{1}{m!}\frac{\text{d}}{\text{d}x^m}\left(\left(x_0 + \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} \varepsilon^i x_i\right)^3\right)(x-a)^m|_{a=0} $$

and $e^{-x}$ as

$$ \varepsilon \left(\text{exp}\left(-\left(x_0 + \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} \varepsilon^i x_i\right)\right)\right) $$

$$ =\varepsilon\sum_{m=0}^\infty \frac{1}{m!}\frac{\text{d}}{\text{d}x^m}\left(\varepsilon \left(\text{exp}\left(-\left(x_0 + \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} \varepsilon^i x_i\right)\right)\right)\right)(x-a)^m|_{a=0} $$

While this may look intimidating in this form, this expression only serves notational purposes. Note that the goal of such expansion is to eventually apply an approximation scheme by truncating higher-ordered terms of $\varepsilon$. Practically, the perturbative expansion follows a series of steps where, at each step, we eliminate these higher-ordered $\varepsilon$ terms. For instance, a first-order perturbative solution allows us to obtain a two-term expansion of the solution. This can be done by expanding the solution only as $x \approx x_0 + \varepsilon x_1$, plugging it into the appropriate Taylor expansion of $x^3$ and $e^{-x}$, and again truncating the higher-ordered $\varepsilon$ retaining only the first order. This linearizes the equation into

$$ x_0^3 + 3\varepsilon x_0^2x_1-\varepsilon e^{-x_0}(1-\varepsilon x_1) + 8 = 0 $$

This process reduces the transcendental algebraic equation into an iterative process of finding solutions for each power of $\varepsilon$ by finding the $x_i$ factors in the expansion $x = x_0 + \sum_{i=1}^{n} \varepsilon^i x_i + \mathcal{O}(\varepsilon^{n-1})$ starting from the unperturbed $\mathcal{O}(\varepsilon^0)$ to the perturbation factor $\mathcal{O}(\varepsilon^n)$ for a $n$th order expansion. That is, for a first-order expansion,

$$ \mathcal{O}(\varepsilon^0): =x_0^3 + 8 = 0 \iff x_0 = -2 $$

$$ \mathcal{O}(\varepsilon^1): =3x_0^2x_1 - e^{-x_0}|_{x_0=-2} = 0 \iff x_1 = e^2/12 $$

giving us a two-term expansion of the solution

$$ x \approx -2 +\varepsilon (e^2/12) $$

While we may never have to solve an algebraic equation this way, such demonstration illustrates the perturbation process: given an equation: may it be algebraic or differential, one can introduce an $n$th order perturbation $\sum_{i=1}^{n} \varepsilon^i x_i$ to a known analytic solution $x_0$ such that for each order in $\varepsilon$, one solves an equation of similar nature to the known analytic solution.

Classical mechanics

 ... close enough to the unperturbed uniform gravitational field

Classical mechanics possess a plethora of events where perturbation theory can be proven useful. The usual problem found in an introductory kinematics course following from Newton’s second law $F_x = m\ddot{x}$ is an approximated differential equation $\ddot{x} = -g$ where $x$ denotes the height of a projectile. However, accounting for the separation dependence of gravitational force, the classically correct way to model a projectile is actually

$$ \ddot{x}(t) = -\frac{gR^2}{(R+x(t))^2}; \hspace{1cm} x(0) = 0, \dot{x}(0) = v_0 $$

Observe that while $\ddot{x} = -g$ is trivial to solve with solution $x_0(t) = -gt^2/2+v_0t$, the unapproximated equation is not. The first key step of perturbing a system is to small parameter $\varepsilon$ from where taking $\varepsilon=0$ reduces the equation to a system with known unperturbed analytic solution. Hence, one can nondimensionalize the above gravitational equation of motion to

$$ \ddot{u}(\tau) = - \frac{1}{(1 + \varepsilon u(\tau))^2} \hspace{1cm} u(0) = 0, \dot{x}(0) = 1 $$

where $\varepsilon = v_0^2/gR$. Observe that this formulation allows us to take the limit of large $R$ such that $\varepsilon$ vanishes and the system reduces to the known analytic solution $x_0(t)$. With these, we can now apply perturbation theory as was discussed. A first order perturbative expansion of the solution

$$ u(\tau) = u_0(\tau) + \varepsilon u_1(\tau) $$

is fed to the Taylor approximation of the equation of motion. The emerging higher-ordered terms in $\varepsilon$ is clipped at each step yielding the reduced differential equation

$$ \ddot{u_0}(\tau) + \varepsilon\ddot{u_1}(\tau) = -1 +2\varepsilon u_0(\tau) $$

with the initial conditions

$$ u_0(0) + \varepsilon u_1(0) = 0; \hspace{1cm} \dot{u}_0(0) + \varepsilon \dot{u}_1(0) = 1 $$

Observe that this forms two differential equations corresponding to $\mathcal{O}(\varepsilon^0)$ and $\mathcal{O}(\varepsilon^1)$ which must be iteratively solved. This gives us a two-term expansion of the solution

$$ u(\tau) = \frac{1}{2} \tau (2 - \tau) + \frac{1}{12} \varepsilon \tau^3 (4-\tau) $$

or

$$ x(t) = \frac{1}{2} t (2v_0 - gt) + \frac{g}{12R} t^4 (4v_0 - gt) $$

In this form, observe that the second term serves as a perturbative correction to the unperturbed analytic solution due to the nonlinear gravitational field.

Quantum mechanics

... close enough to the unperturbed Hamilonian

The state of a quantum system is governed by Schrodinger equation. However, it could only be solved analytically for a very limited special cases: the particle in a box, the harmonic oscillator, and the hydrogen atom. Even if the exact analytic solutions is impossible to solve, the main line of attack is to use perturbation theory by treating the Hamiltonian of interest as a perturbation of one of these existing exact analytic solutions. For instance, knowing a eigenfunction solution $\psi_n^0$ to a Hamiltonian $H^0$ thus solving the time-independent Schrodinger equation

$$ H^0\ket{\psi_n^0} = E_n^0\ket{\psi_n^0} $$

one can treat a new Hamiltonian by considering the first order perturbative expansion of form

$$ H = H^0 + \varepsilon H' $$

and expanding the energy eigenstates and eigenvalues as

$$ \ket{\psi_n} = \sum_{i=0}\varepsilon^i\ket{\psi_n^i} $$

$$ E_n = \sum_{i=0}\varepsilon^i E_n^i $$

yields the fundamental results of a second-order time-independent nondegenerate perturbation theory

$$ (H^0+\varepsilon H’)\left(\psi_0^n+\varepsilon\sum_{m\neq n}\frac{\bra{\psi_m^0}H’\ket{\psi_n^0}}{(E_n^0-E_m^0)}\psi_m^0+…\right) $$

$$ =\left(E_n^0 + \varepsilon\bra{\psi_n^0}H’\ket{\psi_n^0}+\varepsilon^2\sum_{m\neq n}\frac{|\bra{\psi_m^0}H’\ket{\psi_n^0}|^2}{(E_n^0-E_m^0)}\right)\ \left(\psi_0^n+\varepsilon\sum_{m\neq n}\frac{\bra{\psi_m^0}H’\ket{\psi_n^0}}{(E_n^0-E_m^0)}\psi_m^0+…\right) $$

Application-wise, this perturbative expansion may well be the most frequently used equation in quantum mechanics.

General relativity

... close enough to the unperturbed background spacetime

Matter and spacetime geometry is wrapped in the Einstein field equations (EFE)

$$ G_{\mu\nu} := R_{\mu\nu} + \frac{1}{2} R g_{\mu\nu} = \frac{8 \pi G}{c^2} T_{\mu\nu} $$

Although deceptively simple looking, EFE is incredibly difficult to solve and there also only exists a few analytic solutions. Unpacking this from its tensorial form by choosing a coordinate system reveals a set of ten highly nonlinear, coupled, partial differential equation and finding an general exact analytic solution constitute a monstrous task. Nevertheless, like quantum mechanics, there exists only a couple of analytic solutions to several stress-energy tensor $T_{\mu\nu}$. The metric $g_{\mu\nu}$ serves as a potential with physical interpretation of being a spacetime ruler and protractor. An interesting consequence of EFE is that even in the absence of matter ($T_{\mu\nu}=0$), unique, nontrivial analytic solutions still exists. Of course, an example vacuum solutions would be a flat or Minkowski metric $g_{\mu\nu} = \eta_{\mu\nu}$ of form

$$ \text{d}s^2 = - \text{d}t^2 + \text{d}x^2 + \text{d}y^2 + \text{d}z^2 $$

in the Cartesian coordinates {${t,x,y,z}$} reproducing all special relativistic phenomena such as time dilation and length contraction. Another is the Schwarzschild metric of form

$$ \text{d}s^2 = -(1-2GM/r)\text{d}t^2 + (1-2GM/r)^{-1}\text{d}r^2 + r^2(\text{d}\theta^2 + \text{sin}^2\theta\text{d}\phi^2) $$

in the spherical coordinates {$t,r,\theta,\phi$} derived by assuming a spherically-symmetric spacetime. Such spacetime is called a Schwarzschild black hole: one of the most important predictions of relativity with no Newtonian counterpart.

Another is the emergence of gravitational waves in analogy with Maxwellian electromagnetic waves associated with accelerated charges. This gives us another source of information regarding cosmic events. Gravitational waves can be derived by considering a weak-field limit allowing us to decompose the metric as some perturbation from Minkowski. One can apply perturbation methods by introducing a perturbation metric $h_{\mu\nu}$ from a known background metric $g_{\mu\nu}^{(0)}$ such that

$$ g_{\mu\nu} = g_{\mu\nu}^{(0)} + h_{\mu\nu} $$

Choosing a flat background metric $g_{\mu\nu}^{(0)} = \eta_{\mu\nu}$, the perturbed metric $g_{\mu\nu} = \eta_{\mu\nu} + h_{\mu\nu}$ represents a weak field approximation. Plugging this and clipping all higher-ordered terms in $h_{\mu\nu}$ yields the Einstein tensor of form

$$ G_{\mu\nu}=\frac{1}{2}(\partial_\sigma\partial_\nu h^\sigma_\mu + \partial_\sigma\partial_\mu h^\sigma_\nu -\partial_\mu\partial_\nu h - \square h_{\mu\nu} - \eta_{\mu\nu}\partial_\rho\partial_\lambda h^{\rho\lambda} + \eta_{\mu\nu}\square h) $$

which can be expressed in the trace-reversed perturbation $\hbar = h_{\mu\nu}-\eta_{\mu\nu}h/2$ as

$$ \square\hbar_{\mu\nu} = -16\pi T_{\mu\nu} $$

such a wave equation contains the solution of gravitational waves as metric perturbation from a flat spacetime.
